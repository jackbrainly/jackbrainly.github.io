2. why people hating to bashar Al Assad?​


Answer:

The short answer is that he got in the way of the geopolitical machinations of the CIA and Mossad. Most people in the USA and Europe seem to hate Assad mostly because the media tells them he’s a brutal dictator that is power mad and all about living like a king. Of course this narrative does not add up if you really do your research, but that’s what most folks accept as a fact.

A couple of important things to remember about Bashar Assad:

1) He never intended to be president. His older brother was groomed from a young age to be president and his father mostly ignored him. He left Syria to go to England to study optometry because his father didn’t really give him any sort of position in the leadership. When his older brother died unexpectedly in a car accident, Bashar was recalled to Syria by the legislature(and the Ba’ath Party of Syria in particular).

2) His government is much more moderate and multicultural than that of his father. He has become well loved inside Syria as a protector of refugees, a defender of minorities, and yet still someone who shows respect to Islamic culture. He’s even viewed as something of a reformer despite a significant amount of people who have hoped his reforms would go further.

3) He and his wife(who is AWESOME btw) were offered massive bribes and assurances of safety to sell out their country and flee into a luxurious exile. They refused this even though it meant they faced grave danger. It even meant that when his wife was suffering cancer she had a very hard time getting modern medicine to treat it(thankfully she pulled through and is in remission now).

4) He has a female vice president. As just one more sign of how progressive and secular his administration is, his government is very gender-inclusive.

5) In the last presidential election, Assad made the incredible offer to allow all Syrians living in exile around the world to vote. The USA and most NATO allies refused to allow Syrian expats to vote, despite all the rhetoric about bringing democracy to Syria.

6) Despite tensions between his government and certain factions such as the Kurds and certain Sunni factions; Assad’s government has had a very strong core of loyalists from both populations for the entire war. There have been a few clashes between the SAA and the Kurds, but most of those problems have been patched up. Recently, Assad ordered the SAA to restore two abandoned bases near the Northern border in a joint effort between Syria, Iran, Russia, and the Kurds of Rojava. Despite the absolutely bewildering web of loyalties in Syria, Assad has proven to be quite gracious in welcoming estranged factions back into the Syrian fold. He has not made any efforts to punish the Kurds for establishing their own state, and in fact seems to be quite understanding about why they did it(ISIS completely cut them off from the rest of Syria for 2 years).

There is so much more to cover about the complex situation in Syria, and Assad definitely isn’t perfect. As an outsider I find it a bit awkward to see people holding up giant images of their leaders. It seems a bit cultish, but by the standards of the region Syria is practically utopia. I don’t think that imposing outside views of how Syria should be lead is any of my business, but there’s quite a few in the West who feel otherwise. Certainly the corporate media has pushed a narrative crafted by Israel and big oil companies who care a lot more about the geopolitics of the oil and gas market rather than human lives.
