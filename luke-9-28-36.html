1. why did peter want to put up three tents? At Luke 9:28-36​


Answer:

The mistake is by no means an obsolete one. Men are still attempting to make tabernacles, one for Christ, one for Confucius, one for Buddha. Beware of such blasphemy.

Explanation:

How strangely confused Peter was is evident by his suggestion. Imagine making tabernacles for Moses and Elijah, to say nothing of the Master. Had he said, "Let us stay here and make three tabernacles, one for You, and one for me, and one for James and John," it would have had more of a reason in it. What did Moses and Elijah want with tabernacles? The word tabernacle simply means a booth, or boughs made into a shelter for present use. Peter's suggestion was that he should go to the trees and bear back boughs with which to construct three temporary resting-places. Think of Moses sojourning in a tabernacle or Elijah settling down to rest in a booth. The whole suggestion is bizarre. "He knew not what to answer," and for him, as for all men in like circumstances, it was infinitely better to say nothing. He had lost the sense of the spiritual, and his mind, moving wholly within the realm of material things, imagined that the spirits of the just made perfect could find shelter in tabernacles constructed of boughs.

There was, however, a darker side to the mistake of Peter. When he suggested building three tabernacles - one for the Master, one for Moses, and one for Elijah - he seems to have been forgetful of his own confession made but eight days before. Jesus had asked, "Whom do men say that the Son of Man is?" and had received the reply, "Some say John the Baptist, some, Elijah; and others, Jeremiah, or one of the prophets." Then, in answer to His second question, "Who do you say that I am?" Peter had placed his Lord in a position far higher than that of Elijah, "You are the Christ, the Son of the living God." Yet now he suggests making a tabernacle for Jesus, and one for Moses, and one for Elijah, thus putting his Master upon the same level with these men of the past.

18. Gospel reflection about luke 18:9-14


“…For everyone who exalts himself will be humbled but he who humbles himself, will be exalted” – Luke 18:14

REFLECTION – “You know what our divine Saviour, who is very truth and goodness, said to His disciples: “Unless your justice abound more than that (…) of the Pharisees, you shall not enter the kingdom of heaven” (Mt 5:20). These words are truly those of Christ. He who would not condemn the woman taken in adultery; who vouchsafed to speak to the Samaritan woman and reveal heavenly mysteries to her in spite of her guilty life; He who consented to eat with the publicans, socially disqualified as sinners; who allowed Magdalen to wash His feet and wipe them with the hairs if her head; He who was so “meek and humble of heart” (Mt 11:29), publicly hurled anathemas at the Pharisees: “Woe to you (…) hypocrites, you will not enter the kingdom of heaven” (Mt 23:13). (…)

Call to mind the Pharisee whom Christ depicts going up to the Temple to pray. What is his prayer? “My God, I am a man altogether irreproachable; I fast, I give tithes (Lk 18:11-12); You cannot find me in fault on any point; You ought to be proud of me.” And in the literal sense, what he said was true – he did observe all these things.

However, what judgement does Jesus pass upon him? This man went out of the Temple without being justified, his heart empty of God’s grace. Why this condemnation? Because the unhappy man glorified himself, for his good actions and placed all his perfection, in merely outward observance, without troubling himself about the inward dispositions of his heart. Therefore, our Lord tells us: “Unless your justice is greater than that of the Pharisees, you will have no part in the Kingdom of heaven.” (…) It is in the heart that perfection lies; for love is the supreme law.” – Bl Columba Marmion (1858-1923) Abbot – The “instruments of good works” (Christ, the Ideal of the Monk)


19. luke 11:28 reflection and how can you apply this in your everyday life please.​


Answer:

"But he said, Yea rather, blessed are they hear the word of God, and keep it." (Luke 11:28)

The Bible tells us that we are blessed if we hear the word of God and keep it, that is everything we heard from the word of God should be put into action. Hearing the word of God is not enough if we don't follow it. The Book of Revelation also tells us that keeping the words of God will help us to prepare ourselves for the coming of the Lord:

"Blessed is he that readeth, that they hear the words of this prophecy, and keep those things which are written therein: for the time is at hand." (Revelation 1:3)

As a Christian and follower of Christ, hearing His words and keeping them helps us to become more like Christ.

"Let this mind be in you, which was also in Christ Jesus." (Philippians 2:5)

God's word is our ultimate guide in this sinful world. We are the light of the world, and by keeping His words, we are reflecting Christ's righteousness to brighten up the world.

As a student who hears the word of God, I will put it inside my heart, for I believe that keeping His words will make me a better person and follower of Christ. I will obey my parents for it is one of the commandments of God. I will become a responsible individual in my community that respects the authorities. I will become a living Bible to all of my neighbors so that they can see how blessed is the person if he has God in his life.

23. lessons from luke 18:9-14​


Answer:

There are several lessons that can be drawn from the passage in Luke 18:9-14. Here are a few potential interpretations:

Humility is important: In this passage, the tax collector acknowledges his own sinfulness and asks for God's mercy, while the Pharisee boasts about his own righteousness. The tax collector is ultimately justified, while the Pharisee is not. This suggests that humility is an important virtue and that it is important to recognize our own limitations and faults.

God is merciful: The tax collector is justified not because of his own merit, but because he seeks God's mercy and forgiveness. This suggests that God is a merciful and forgiving God, who is willing to extend grace to those who turn to him.

Pride can be a hindrance: The Pharisee's pride in his own righteousness ultimately leads to his own downfall. This suggests that pride can be a hindrance to one's spiritual growth and that it is important to recognize our own limitations and faults.

God sees the heart: In this passage, it is not the outward appearance or actions of the tax collector or the Pharisee that matter, but rather their heart and their relationship with God. This suggests that God sees and knows the heart, and that it is important to be sincere in our faith.

Overall, these lessons highlight the importance of humility, recognizing our own sinfulness, seeking God's mercy, and being sincere in our faith.
